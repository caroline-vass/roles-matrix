How we assess goals at Festina Group Tech
The principle
Goals are not a pass/fail checklist — they are evidence of how someone is growing. A goal that was attempted but didn't fully land, where the person can articulate why and what they learned, is more valuable than a goal that was gamed to show green. Assessors should look for consistent demonstrated behaviour, not one-off moments.
Rating scale
1 — Not demonstrated
The goal was not meaningfully attempted, or the behaviour is absent even when opportunities arose. No evidence of progress toward the linked competency.
2 — Developing
Partial progress. The person is working toward the goal but not yet consistent. Shows awareness and intent but needs more reps or more depth.
3 — Achieved
The goal was met with clear evidence. The behaviour is consistent and visible to others. The linked competency has measurably improved.
4 — Exceeded
The goal was met and the person went further — in scope, impact, or by lifting others. Evidence of performing at the next level.
Assessment cycle
1
Goal-setting
Employee and manager align on 3–5 goals, each mapped to a competency and level transition
2
Mid-cycle check-in
Informal 1:1 review. Flag goals needing adjustment. No rating yet.
3
Self-assessment
Employee rates each goal 1–4 with evidence. Prompts reflection, not justification.
4
Manager review
Manager rates independently, then calibrates with employee. Gaps become the conversation.
What counts as evidence?
Evidence should be specific and observable — not "I communicated well" but "I wrote the team's onboarding doc and three people have referenced it." Good evidence includes: work artefacts others can point to, feedback from peers or stakeholders, decisions made and their outcomes, situations navigated and what happened as a result.
Linking goal ratings to level progression
No single goal rating triggers a promotion. Level progression requires a pattern across multiple cycles showing consistent performance at the next level. Someone consistently scoring 3–4 on goals tagged to their next transition, across two or more review cycles, is a strong candidate for progression.
The Lead designation and goals
Taking a Lead role is itself a goal-worthy stretch assignment at any level. When someone takes on Lead responsibilities, their goals should reflect the planning and coordination behaviours — not just delivery. A Lead Junior or Lead Mid should have at least one goal explicitly tied to the planning scope they are owning.
